---
title: Deploy Hooks
---

 <p>A deploy hook lets your static site rebuild automatically whenever theStacc publishes, updates, or unpublishes a blog post. Without one, you'd have to log in to your hosting platform and trigger a rebuild by hand every time your content changes.</p>
<p>It's a small piece of setup that you do once. After that, fresh content goes live on your site on its own.</p>
<h2 id="how-deploy-hooks-work">How deploy hooks work<a aria-label="Copy link to How deploy hooks work" class="heading-anchor" href="#how-deploy-hooks-work">#</a></h2>
<p>A deploy hook is just a special URL that your hosting platform gives you. When something POSTs to that URL, your platform starts a new build. theStacc stores that URL on your project and POSTs to it for you at the right moments.</p>
<p>Here's the full flow:</p>
<ol><li>A blog is published (or updated, or unpublished) in theStacc.</li><li>theStacc sends a POST request to your deploy hook URL.</li><li>Your hosting platform receives the request and starts a new build.</li><li>During the build, your site fetches the latest content from the <a href="/docs/developers/public-blog-api/">Public Blog API</a>.</li><li>New static HTML pages are generated and deployed.</li></ol>
<p>The whole thing usually takes one to three minutes, depending on how long your site takes to build.</p>
<h2 id="a-build-trigger-not-a-publishing-destination">A build trigger, not a publishing destination<a aria-label="Copy link to A build trigger, not a publishing destination" class="heading-anchor" href="#a-build-trigger-not-a-publishing-destination">#</a></h2>
<p>It's worth being clear about what a deploy hook is and isn't, because it's easy to confuse with your publishing integration.</p>
<ul><li>A <strong>publishing integration</strong> (WordPress, a custom webhook, and so on) is a <strong>destination</strong> — it's where theStacc sends the actual blog content.</li><li>A <strong>deploy hook</strong> is a <strong>build trigger</strong> — it carries no content. It only tells your hosting platform "something changed, please rebuild," and your site then pulls the content itself from the Public Blog API.</li></ul>
<p>The two are completely separate and can be used together. A static site typically uses the <a href="/docs/developers/public-blog-api/">Public Blog API</a> to read content plus a deploy hook to know when to rebuild. You do not need a publishing integration for the deploy hook to fire.</p>
<h2 id="events-that-trigger-a-rebuild">Events that trigger a rebuild<a aria-label="Copy link to Events that trigger a rebuild" class="heading-anchor" href="#events-that-trigger-a-rebuild">#</a></h2>
<p>theStacc fires your deploy hook when:</p>
<ul><li>A blog is <strong>published</strong>.</li><li>A published blog is <strong>updated</strong> and re-synced.</li><li>A published blog is <strong>unpublished</strong> (removed from your site).</li></ul>
<p>Draft edits, content generation, and approval steps do <strong>not</strong> trigger a rebuild. The hook only fires for changes that affect what visitors see on your live site.</p>
<p>Each event fires the hook immediately, as a background task. If several blogs go live close together (for example, when you approve a batch), each one fires the hook, so your hosting platform may queue more than one build. Most platforms handle this gracefully by superseding older queued builds with the newest one.</p>
<h2 id="setting-up-a-deploy-hook">Setting up a deploy hook<a aria-label="Copy link to Setting up a deploy hook" class="heading-anchor" href="#setting-up-a-deploy-hook">#</a></h2>
<p>There are two parts: get a hook URL from your hosting platform, then paste it into theStacc.</p>
<h3 id="step-1-get-your-deploy-hook-url">Step 1: Get your deploy hook URL<a aria-label="Copy link to Step 1: Get your deploy hook URL" class="heading-anchor" href="#step-1-get-your-deploy-hook-url">#</a></h3>
<p>#### Cloudflare Pages</p>
<ol><li>Open your project in the Cloudflare dashboard.</li><li>Go to <strong>Settings &gt; Builds &amp; deployments &gt; Deploy hooks</strong>.</li><li>Click <strong>Add deploy hook</strong>.</li><li>Name it "theStacc" and select your production branch.</li><li>Copy the generated URL (it looks like <code>https://api.cloudflare.com/client/v4/pages/webhooks/deploy_hooks/...</code>).</li></ol>
<p>#### Vercel</p>
<ol><li>Open your project in the Vercel dashboard.</li><li>Go to <strong>Settings &gt; Git &gt; Deploy Hooks</strong>.</li><li>Create a hook named "theStacc" for your production branch.</li><li>Copy the generated URL (it looks like <code>https://api.vercel.com/v1/integrations/deploy/...</code>).</li></ol>
<p>#### Netlify</p>
<ol><li>Open your site in the Netlify dashboard.</li><li>Go to <strong>Site configuration &gt; Build &amp; deploy &gt; Build hooks</strong>.</li><li>Click <strong>Add build hook</strong>.</li><li>Name it "theStacc" and select your production branch.</li><li>Copy the generated URL (it looks like <code>https://api.netlify.com/build_hooks/...</code>).</li></ol>

<p>Because theStacc sends a plain POST (not a GitHub dispatch event), you'll need a small bridge — for example a Cloudflare Worker or a similar webhook-to-dispatch service — that converts the incoming POST into a GitHub repository dispatch. Point your deploy hook URL in theStacc at that bridge.</p>
<h3 id="step-2-add-the-url-to-thestacc">Step 2: Add the URL to theStacc<a aria-label="Copy link to Step 2: Add the URL to theStacc" class="heading-anchor" href="#step-2-add-the-url-to-thestacc">#</a></h3>
<ol><li>Go to <strong>Settings &gt; Publishing &gt; API Access</strong> in your theStacc dashboard.</li><li>Find the <strong>Deploy Hook</strong> field.</li><li>Paste the full URL you copied. It must start with <code>https://</code>.</li><li>Click <strong>Save</strong>.</li><li>Click <strong>Test Hook</strong> to confirm the connection. This sends a real POST to your URL and starts a test build on your hosting platform.</li></ol>
<p>Once a hook is saved, the <strong>Test Hook</strong> and <strong>Remove</strong> buttons appear so you can re-test or clear it at any time.</p>
<blockquote><p>The Deploy Hook field is visible to project admins only, because the URL is effectively a secret that can trigger your build pipeline.</p></blockquote>
<h2 id="build-optimization">Build optimization<a aria-label="Copy link to Build optimization" class="heading-anchor" href="#build-optimization">#</a></h2>
<p>Each deploy hook triggers a full site rebuild, so it pays to keep builds fast:</p>
<ul><li><strong>Cache API responses.</strong> If your framework supports build caching, the Public Blog API returns standard caching headers you can take advantage of.</li><li><strong>Use incremental builds.</strong> Frameworks like Next.js and Astro can regenerate only new or changed pages instead of the whole site.</li><li><strong>Avoid unnecessary rebuilds.</strong> theStacc only fires the hook for published-content changes, never for drafts or internal edits, so your build queue stays clean by default.</li></ul>
<h2 id="monitoring-builds">Monitoring builds<a aria-label="Copy link to Monitoring builds" class="heading-anchor" href="#monitoring-builds">#</a></h2>
<p>After you've set up a hook, do one end-to-end check:</p>
<ol><li>Publish a test blog in theStacc.</li><li>Open your hosting platform's build logs — you should see a new build start within seconds.</li><li>Wait for the build to finish, then open your site and confirm the new blog appears.</li><li>Load the published URL in your browser and check that the content renders correctly.</li></ol>
<p>From then on, your hosting platform's build history is the source of truth for whether a rebuild ran.</p>
<h2 id="troubleshooting">Troubleshooting<a aria-label="Copy link to Troubleshooting" class="heading-anchor" href="#troubleshooting">#</a></h2>
<p>If a publish doesn't seem to trigger a build, work through these:</p>
<ul><li><strong>Re-run Test Hook.</strong> In <strong>Settings &gt; Publishing &gt; API Access</strong>, click <strong>Test Hook</strong>. It reports back whether the request succeeded and the HTTP status your platform returned. A failure message tells you whether theStacc couldn't connect, timed out, or got a non-success status.</li><li><strong>Check the URL.</strong> It must be a complete, public <code>https://</code> URL copied directly from your hosting platform. theStacc rejects URLs that are too short, contain spaces, or point at private or internal addresses, so a partial paste won't save.</li><li><strong>Confirm the hook still exists on your platform.</strong> If you deleted or regenerated the hook in Cloudflare, Vercel, or Netlify, the old URL stops working — generate a new one and update it in theStacc.</li><li><strong>Check that automatic builds aren't disabled</strong> on your hosting platform, and that any environment variables your build needs (such as your Public Blog API key) are set there.</li></ul>
<p>One important detail: <strong>deploy hooks do not auto-retry.</strong> theStacc fires the hook once per qualifying event in the background. If that single request fails — your platform was down, the URL was wrong, the request timed out — theStacc logs it and moves on; it does not try again, and the failure never blocks or breaks your publish in theStacc. To recover from a missed build, fix the cause and either re-test the hook or trigger a manual rebuild on your hosting platform.</p>
<h2 id="security">Security<a aria-label="Copy link to Security" class="heading-anchor" href="#security">#</a></h2>
<p>Your deploy hook URL is stored securely and is only ever shown to project admins — it's never exposed in the public part of the app. The URL itself is the credential: anyone who has it can start a build, so treat it like a password.</p>
<p>theStacc also validates the URL before saving it and again before firing it, and refuses to send requests to private or internal network addresses.</p>
<p>If you think your URL has leaked:</p>
<ol><li>Delete the hook on your hosting platform.</li><li>Create a new one.</li><li>Paste the new URL into <strong>Settings &gt; Publishing &gt; API Access</strong> and save.</li></ol>
