## What Is NAP?

NAP (Name, Address, Phone number) is the standardized trio of business information that search engines cross-reference across websites, directories, and profiles to verify a business is real and determine where to rank it in local results.

Think of NAP as your business's digital fingerprint. Google, Bing, and Apple Maps constantly compare your NAP across hundreds of sources. Your [Google Business Profile](/glossary/google-business-profile), your website, Yelp, Facebook, Yellow Pages, industry directories, and more. When the data matches everywhere, Google trusts you. When it doesn't, things get messy.

BrightLocal's research shows that 80% of consumers lose trust in a local business if they find incorrect contact information online. And from Google's side, inconsistent NAP data is one of the most common reasons businesses fail to rank in the [Local Pack](/glossary/local-pack). Simple concept. Enormous impact.

## Why Does NAP Matter?

NAP consistency is the foundation of [local SEO](/glossary/local-seo). Without it, everything else you do. Reviews, GBP posts, local content. Sits on a shaky base.

- **Google uses NAP to verify your business exists**. Consistent NAP across multiple sources acts as a trust signal. It tells Google "this business is real, it's at this address, and it's reachable at this number."
- **Inconsistencies confuse search engines**. If your Google Business Profile says "123 Main St" but Yelp says "123 Main Street, Suite 4," Google can't be sure they're the same business. That uncertainty hurts your rankings.
- **Wrong phone numbers lose you customers**. A Moz study found that 93% of consumers are frustrated by incorrect information in online directories. Wrong numbers don't just hurt SEO. They directly cost you leads.
- **NAP feeds the [citation](/glossary/citation) ecosystem**. Every directory listing, social profile, and business mention builds a citation. If your NAP is wrong in one place, it often propagates as data aggregators share it.

Any business competing for local search visibility needs to treat NAP consistency as non-negotiable.

## How NAP Works

NAP seems simple. It's just 3 fields. But the mechanics of how search engines use it are more nuanced than most people realize.

### Cross-Referencing Across Sources

Google doesn't just read your GBP listing. It crawls thousands of directories, websites, and data sources, extracting NAP information and comparing it. When it finds the same business name, same address, and same phone number across dozens of sources, confidence goes up. The more consistent matches, the stronger the signal.

### Exact Match Matters

"Bob's Plumbing LLC" and "Bob's Plumbing" are not the same in Google's eyes. Neither are "St." and "Street," or "(555) 123-4567" and "555-123-4567." While Google has gotten better at fuzzy matching, exact consistency is still the safest approach. Pick one format for your business name, address, and phone number. Then use it identically everywhere.

### Data Aggregators Spread Your NAP

Four major data aggregators in the US. Data Axle, Localeze, Foursquare, and Factual. Supply business information to hundreds of smaller directories. If your NAP is wrong in an aggregator, that error multiplies across dozens of sites. Fixing it at the source is the most efficient way to clean up widespread inconsistencies.

### NAP on Your Website

Your own website should display your NAP in a crawlable format. Not buried in an image or JavaScript widget. Best practice: include it in your footer on every page and on a dedicated contact page. Using [LocalBusiness schema markup](/glossary/localbusiness-schema) helps Google parse it cleanly.

## Types of NAP Issues

NAP problems fall into predictable categories:

- **Business name variations**. Using "LLC," "Inc.," or abbreviations inconsistently. "Smith & Jones Legal" on your GBP but "Smith and Jones Legal Group" on Yelp.
- **Address format mismatches** ,  "Ste" vs "Suite," "St" vs "Street," missing apartment or unit numbers. Seems trivial, but creates confusion at scale.
- **Old phone numbers**. Businesses change phone numbers and forget to update 30+ directory listings. The old number lives on across the web.
- **Moved locations**. The #1 cause of major NAP inconsistency. Your old address persists on directories you forgot about for months or years.
- **Duplicate listings**. Two Google Business Profile listings for the same business with slightly different NAP. This actively competes against yourself in local rankings.

Most NAP issues stem from one event: something changed and wasn't updated everywhere. That's why regular [citation audits](/glossary/citation-audit) matter.

## NAP Examples

**Example 1: Dental practice with inconsistent name**
A dental practice operates as "Bright Smiles Family Dentistry" but their GBP lists "Bright Smiles Dentistry," Yelp shows "Bright Smiles Family Dental," and their website says "Bright Smiles Family Dentistry, PLLC." Three variations across 3 platforms. After standardizing to one exact name everywhere and submitting corrections to data aggregators, they saw a 2-position jump in the Local Pack within 8 weeks.

**Example 2: Plumber with old phone number**
A plumbing company switched phone providers and got a new number. They updated their website and GBP but forgot about 40+ directory listings still showing the old number. Potential customers calling the old number got a disconnected message. A [citation cleanup](/glossary/citation-cleanup) project fixed the directories. And the company's GBP call volume increased 25% in the following month.

**Example 3: Law firm that moved offices**
A law firm relocated across town but didn't update their listings beyond Google and their website. For 6 months, Apple Maps, Bing Places, and industry directories showed the old address. Google's confidence in the firm's location data dropped, and they fell out of the Local Pack for their most valuable keyword. Full NAP cleanup restored their position.

## NAP vs Citation

People sometimes use NAP and citation interchangeably. They're related but different.

| | NAP | [Citation](/glossary/citation) |
|---|---|---|
| **What it is** | The data itself (name, address, phone) | A mention of your business containing NAP data |
| **Where it lives** | Everywhere your business info appears | Specific directories, websites, and profiles |
| **Focus** | Consistency and accuracy of the data | Volume and quality of mentions |
| **Example** | "Bob's Plumbing, 123 Main St, (555) 123-4567" | A Yelp listing containing Bob's Plumbing NAP data |

NAP is the information. A citation is a container for that information. You need both. Consistent NAP data inside a strong network of citations.

## NAP Best Practices

- **Create a NAP master document**. Write your official business name, address, and phone number in one document. Character-for-character. Every listing you create or update should copy from this document exactly.
- **Audit your citations quarterly**. Use tools like BrightLocal, Moz Local, or Whitespark to scan for NAP inconsistencies across the web. Fix errors at the source. Especially data aggregators.
- **Add [schema markup](/glossary/localbusiness-schema) to your website**. LocalBusiness structured data on your site helps Google parse your NAP directly from your own authoritative source. Include it on your homepage and contact page.
- **Monitor for duplicate GBP listings**. Duplicate [Google Business Profile](/glossary/google-business-profile) listings with slightly different NAP confuse Google. Merge or remove duplicates through GBP's reporting tools.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What does NAP stand for?

NAP stands for Name, Address, and Phone number. These three data points form the core identity of a local business online. Some practitioners expand it to NAP+W, adding Website URL as a fourth element.

### Why does NAP consistency matter for SEO?

Consistent NAP across the web tells Google your business information is trustworthy. Inconsistencies create doubt about your business's identity and location, which directly hurts your chances of appearing in the [Local Pack](/glossary/local-pack) and [local organic results](/glossary/local-organic-results).

### How do I find NAP inconsistencies?

Run a citation audit using tools like BrightLocal, Moz Local, or Whitespark. They scan major directories and flag listings where your name, address, or phone number doesn't match your Google Business Profile. Manual checks on the top 20 directories also work for a quick audit.

### How often should I check my NAP?

Quarterly audits are the minimum. Check immediately after any change to your business name, address, phone number, or office location. Data aggregators can reintroduce old data, so ongoing monitoring catches regressions.
